## 1. GnRH agonist trigger — suboptimal response and risk factors

### ★ Gambini et al. (2024) — Risk factors for poor oocyte yield and oocyte immaturity after GnRH agonist triggering

**Human Reproduction**, Volume 39, Issue 5, May 2024, Pages 963–973
DOI: 10.1093/humrep/deae041

**Why it matters for the case**: This is the most directly relevant recent paper. It identifies which patient and cycle characteristics predict a poor response to agonist-only triggering — exactly the failure mode that occurred. The authors note that low LH levels measured *after* the trigger correlate with poor oocyte recuperation, raising the question of whether post-trigger LH was checked at all in the failed cycle.

**Key findings paraphrased**: Lower ovarian reserve and lower post-trigger LH levels were the principal risk factors for poor oocyte recuperation. Higher BMI and higher AMH were risk factors for poor oocyte maturation. The authors recommend identifying at-risk patients and considering rescue hCG or dual trigger strategies.

[PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38503490/) · [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/humrep/article/39/5/963/7624167)

---

### ★ Prediction, assessment, and management of suboptimal GnRH agonist trigger: a systematic review

**Journal of Assisted Reproduction and Genetics**, 2022
DOI: 10.1007/s10815-021-02359-y

**Why it matters**: Defines the threshold most commonly used in the literature for diagnosing a suboptimal agonist trigger response — post-trigger LH <15 IU/L — which best correlates with empty follicle syndrome and poor oocyte recovery. Useful framing for the complaint: was post-trigger LH measured? If not, was the suboptimal response detectable in time to rescue?

**Key findings paraphrased**: Across 37 included studies, low basal FSH/LH/E2, low LH on trigger day, prolonged stimulation, and increased gonadotropin requirements all correlated with suboptimal response. Long-term oral contraceptive use and BMI extremes were also identified. Re-triggering with hCG is described as a viable rescue when post-trigger LH is low.

[PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35037119/) · [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10815-021-02359-y)

---

### Follicular GnRH agonist challenge test (FACT) to predict suboptimal response

**Scientific Reports**, March 2024
DOI: 10.1038/s41598-024-56418-2

A pre-cycle screening tool: a 0.2 mg agonist injection on cycle day 2, with LH measured 10–12 hours later, predicted post-trigger LH levels. Suggests there are predictive tools that *could* identify patients at risk of suboptimal agonist response before the cycle starts.

[Article](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-56418-2)

## 2. Dual trigger vs single trigger — efficacy and reproductive outcomes

### Huang et al. (2023) — Dual trigger improves pregnancy rate in fresh IVF cycles vs hCG trigger alone: meta-analysis of RCTs

**Journal of Assisted Reproduction and Genetics**, 2023
DOI: 10.1007/s10815-023-02893-x

Systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised trials. Concludes dual trigger (GnRHa + hCG) improves pregnancy outcomes in fresh transfers compared with hCG alone. Earlier meta-analyses had been equivocal; this one finds a clear signal once additional RCTs are included.

[PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37466846/) · [Full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10440333/)

---

### Frontiers narrative review (2025) — Dual triggering for final oocyte maturation

**Frontiers in Endocrinology**, April 2025
DOI: 10.3389/fendo.2025.1556732

**Useful nuance**: Reviews dual triggering across responder phenotypes. Argues benefits are clearest in poor and suboptimal responders, with no significant differences from hCG alone in normal/hyper-responders. Helps frame which patient profiles benefit most — and cautions against over-claiming benefit in healthy responders.

[Full text](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2025.1556732/full)

---

### Mahmoud et al. (2025) — Dual vs single ovulation triggers in IVF/ICSI: systematic review and meta-analysis

ASIDE Health Sciences, 2025

Searched up to January 2025. Concludes dual trigger significantly improves clinical pregnancy and live birth rates over hCG alone. Useful as the most up-to-date pooled estimate.

[Article](https://asidejournals.com/index.php/Health-Sciences/article/view/155)

---

### Network meta-analysis — hCG vs agonist vs dual vs double trigger in healthy responders

**Fertility and Sterility**, November 2025 (abstract)

Network meta-analysis of 12 RCTs (1,931 women) comparing hCG, GnRH agonist, dual, and double triggers in predicted healthy responders. The full paper, when published, should give the cleanest comparison across the four strategies.

[ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0015028224023896)

## 3. Premature ovulation prevention

### Flexible short antagonist protocol with GnRH-ant added on trigger day for poor responders

**Archives of Gynecology and Obstetrics**, 2024
DOI: 10.1007/s00404-023-07287-z

Adding a GnRH antagonist on trigger day effectively suppressed hidden LH peaks and prevented spontaneous premature ovulation in poor responders, without affecting clinical pregnancy or live birth rates. A simple, evidence-based safety net — relevant to whether monitoring + adjunctive antagonist would have prevented premature ovulation in the failed cycle.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10808440/)

---

### Single-dose GnRH antagonist after hCG trigger in diminished ovarian reserve patients

**Revista Brasileira de Ginecologia e Obstetrícia / RBGO**, 2022
DOI: 10.1055/s-0041-1736297

Adds to evidence that a single antagonist dose post-trigger reduces premature luteinisation events.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9948117/)

## 4. Lead follicle size and trigger timing

### ★ Abbara et al. — Follicle size on day of trigger most likely to yield a mature oocyte

**Frontiers in Endocrinology**, 2018
DOI: 10.3389/fendo.2018.00193

Foundational paper. Across hCG, GnRHa, and kisspeptin triggers, follicles of **12–19 mm on the morning of trigger** contributed most to mature oocyte yield. Follicles outside that window — including notably large ones — produced fewer mature oocytes. Directly relevant to the 30 mm follicle issue, since post-mature follicles drift outside the optimal range and contribute proportionally less.

[Full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5930292/)

---

### Romanski et al. — Optimal follicle size at ovulatory trigger in cycles with letrozole

**Fertility and Sterility**, 2021

Lead-follicle size analysis in letrozole co-administration cycles. In standard antagonist cycles without letrozole, the broader literature converges on lead follicles of 17–22 mm on trigger day; sizes substantially above 22 mm tend to be associated with declining MII rates and post-mature oocytes. (Worth pairing with Wirleitner et al. 2018 in *Ultrasound Obstet Gynecol* on follicle volume and oocyte competence.)

[Article](https://www.fertstert.org/article/S0015-0282(21)01237-1/fulltext)

---

### Vandekerckhove et al. — Delaying trigger by 24 h to maximise MII oocytes

**Reproductive Biology and Endocrinology**, RCT

Delaying trigger by one day in patients meeting size criteria but with low progesterone increased mean MII yield by ~2.4 oocytes. Underscores that trigger-day timing is a meaningful lever — but one that must be *managed*, not allowed to drift past the optimal window.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4008411/)

## 5. ZyMōt and microfluidic sperm selection

### ★ Godiwala et al. (2024) — Zymot vs density gradient: euploidy outcomes RCT

Randomised controlled trial in the general IVF population. Zymot did **not** significantly improve embryo euploidy rates over density gradient centrifugation, but did improve fertilisation rates and reduced sperm DNA fragmentation. Crucial limitation: the study was *not* enriched for couples with high SDF, and the male-factor / RPL subgroups were underpowered.

**Implication for the case**: Zymot's biggest theoretical benefit is in high-SDF or recurrent loss patients — the population it would actually be tested in if the Extensiv test confirmed elevated dsSDF.

[Summary write-up](https://www.remembryo.com/study-assesses-embryo-euploidy-rates-after-zymot-used-to-select-sperm/)

---

### Comparative study: ZyMot-ICSI vs density gradient in unselected IVF/ICSI

**MDPI Life**, 2023

Retrospective study of 239 couples. No significant difference in fertilisation rate, but blastocyst rate differed (worth verifying direction in the full text). Reinforces the picture that, in unselected populations, microfluidic selection has marginal-to-modest benefits over standard preparation.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10145657/)

---

### Active trials worth tracking

## 6. Sperm DNA fragmentation and ART outcomes

### ★ Sperm dsDNA fragmentation by neutral comet assay predicts IVF live birth — multicentre prospective study

**Human Reproduction**, advance article March 2026
DOI: 10.1093/humrep/deag046

**Why it matters**: This is the single most relevant new paper for the **Extensiv** test recommendation. It reports that **double-stranded sperm DNA fragmentation (dsSDF)**, measured by neutral comet assay across three European IVF clinics (March 2021 – October 2024, 302 males plus 126 sperm-donor controls), is a strong, independent predictor of live birth. Single-stranded breaks are linked more to spontaneous conception failure; double-stranded breaks are linked to higher miscarriage rates and lower live birth.

This paper essentially provides the evidence base for testing dsSDF specifically — which is what the Extensiv assay is designed to detect — rather than relying on TUNEL or SCSA alone.

[Oxford Academic (advance article)](https://academic.oup.com/humrep/advance-article/doi/10.1093/humrep/deag046/8554295)

---

### ★ Sperm DNA fragmentation and assisted reproduction: an umbrella meta-analysis

**Published 2025**

Umbrella meta-analysis of meta-analyses on SDF and ART outcomes (clinical pregnancy, pregnancy loss, live birth). Synthesises the entire prior evidence base. AMSTAR-2 and GRADE quality appraisal applied. Useful as a high-level reference for the strength of the SDF-outcome relationship across IVF, ICSI, and IUI.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12870536/)

---

### Yang et al. (2025) — DFI and ART outcomes in 5,271 women

**Frontiers in Endocrinology**, January 2025
DOI: 10.3389/fendo.2024.1530972

Large retrospective cohort (5,271 women, first IVF cycles 2020–2023) stratified into DFI <15%, 15–30%, ≥30%. DFI negatively correlated with semen quality and embryonic development outcomes. Explicitly references the AUA/EAU 2023 guidelines, which now formally recognise SDF testing.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11790627/)

---

### Sperm DNA fragmentation in normozoospermic men: blastocyst formation and quality

**Journal of Clinical Medicine**, 2025

In men with otherwise normal semen analysis, DFI still associated with blastocyst formation rate and quality. Relevant because conventional semen analysis can be reassuring while DFI is not.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12734054/)

## 7. TESE vs ejaculated sperm in non-azoospermic men with high SDF

### ★ Khoo et al. (2024) — Does testicular sperm improve ICSI outcomes for non-azoospermic men with elevated SDF?

**European Urology Focus**, May 2024, 10(3):410–420

Systematic review and meta-analysis. Pregnancy rate significantly higher with testicular than ejaculated sperm (OR 2.51 by SCSA/SCIT, OR 3.65 by TUNEL). Live birth rate also higher (OR 2.59 by SCSA/SCIT). No difference in fertilisation or miscarriage rate. Authors caution evidence is limited by quality and sample size — RCTs still needed.

**Why it matters**: This is the up-to-date evidence base for Prof Yap's TESE recommendation. The effect sizes are clinically meaningful; the caveat is study quality.

[PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37709593/)

---

### Mareca-Coca et al. (2025) — Superior live birth rates with testicular sperm in high-SDF couples

**Biology (MDPI)**, January 2025

More recent systematic review and meta-analysis specifically for couples with high ejaculated SDF. Adds to the case for considering Testi-ICSI when ejaculated SDF is elevated.

[Article](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-7737/14/2/130)

---

### Esteves et al. — Comparison in oligozoospermic men with high SDF (foundational)

**Fertility and Sterility**, 2015

The much-cited 147-couple study showing testicular sperm DFI of 8.3% vs 40.7% in ejaculate, with substantially better clinical pregnancy (51.9% vs 40.2%) and live birth (46.7% vs 26.4%). Still the strongest individual dataset in this area.

[ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0015028215018749)

## 8. PGT-A in advanced maternal age

### ★ Casteleiro Alves et al. (2024) — PGT-A in AMA: multicentre retrospective cohort with propensity matching

**Human Reproduction**, July 2024 (ESHRE abstract O-152)
DOI: 10.1093/humrep/deae108.171

9,328 patients aged >37 undergoing first IVF/ICSI cycle, propensity-matched. Among patients with at least one embryo to transfer, PGT-A improved both per-transfer live birth rate and cumulative live birth rate. Reported CLBR after five embryo transfers was approximately 100% in the PGT-A group vs 74.5% in the control group.

**Caveat baked into the design**: the analysis is restricted to patients with at least one embryo *to transfer*. The Cumitas-style critique — that PGT-A penalises patients with low embryo numbers, since cycles without euploid embryos don't reach transfer — still applies.

[Oxford Academic abstract](https://academic.oup.com/humrep/article/39/Supplement_1/deae108.171/7704015)

---

### ★ ASRM Practice Committee Opinion on PGT-A (2024)

**Fertility and Sterility**, 2024

The current authoritative committee opinion. Notes that benefit signals in AMA exist but most RCTs were performed in favourable-prognosis patients with sufficient blastocysts. Calls for pragmatic ITT trials specifically in AMA.

[ASRM page](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/the-use-of-preimplantation-genetic-testing-for-aneuploidy-a-committee-opinion-2024/)

---

### SART CORS analysis — PGT-A and CLBR across ages

**Journal of Assisted Reproduction and Genetics**, 2023

Large registry analysis (133,494 autologous cycles). PGT-A associated with reduced CLBR at all ages **except >40**, where CLBR was equivalent. The selective benefit pattern at older ages is consistent with — but interpretively distinct from — the propensity-matched study above.

[Full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9840738/)

---

### Yan et al. (2021) — STAR / NEJM RCT on PGT-A vs conventional IVF

**New England Journal of Medicine**, 2021

Multicentre RCT in subfertile women aged 20–37 with three or more good-quality blastocysts. PGT-A did not improve cumulative live birth rate over conventional IVF in this favourable-prognosis group. Often cited as the reference RCT showing PGT-A's null effect when the embryo cohort is large and the patient is young — the antithesis of the AMA case.

[NEJM](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2103613)

## 9. Blastocyst morphology, ICM/TE grade, and live birth

### Gardini et al. (2024) — Trophectoderm is the strongest live-birth predictor in untested blastocyst transfers

**Journal of Clinical Medicine**, November 2024

Retrospective study of 1,546 single-blastocyst FETs. After adjustment, **trophectoderm grade emerged as the strongest morphological predictor of live birth** — outperforming both ICM grade and expansion stage. Tackles the long-running debate over which Gardner sub-score matters most.

[Full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11595274/)

---

### ★ Suzuki et al. (2024) — In euploid embryos, ICM grade predicts live birth

**Reproductive Medicine and Biology**, January 2024

In **euploid** (PGT-A normal) blastocysts, ICM grade was the morphological feature most strongly associated with live birth. Useful counterpoint to the trophectoderm-favouring finding above: once aneuploidy is ruled out, ICM may carry more weight.

[Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/rmb2.12560)

---

### Effect of blastocyst morphology and developmental rate on euploidy and live birth in PGT-A cycles

4,172 blastocysts from 941 PGT-A cycles. Better ICM and TE grades both associated with higher euploidy rates. Day of blastulation (5 vs 6 vs 7) did not independently predict euploidy.

[Full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12041180/)

---

### Inner cell mass grade and earlier blastulation associated with pregnancy in euploid embryos

**Reproductive Sciences**, September 2025

Single-centre cohort of 768 euploid FETs (52.6% live birth). Better ICM grade and earlier blastulation both associated with higher live birth odds.

[Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43032-025-01971-y)

## 10. MTHFR, methylation, and IVF outcomes

### ★ Lu et al. (2022) — MTHFR C677T/A1298C combination polymorphisms and IVF/ICSI outcomes

**Human Reproduction Open**, December 2022
DOI: 10.1093/hropen/hoac055

Retrospective cohort study examining MTHFR C677T/A1298C combinations and IVF/ICSI outcomes. Mechanistic discussion includes reduced MTHFR enzyme activity disrupting folliculogenesis via DNA/protein synthesis suppression and altered oocyte methylation patterns. Provides the biological framing for personalising folate supplementation.

[Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/hropen/article/2023/1/hoac055/6873744)

---

### MTHFR 677TT and reduced cumulative live birth rate in GnRHa short protocol

**Reproductive Biology and Endocrinology**, 2022

Retrospective cohort of 1,173 women in first IVF-ET. MTHFR 677TT genotype associated with fewer embryos and lower CLBR — most pronounced in patients on short agonist protocols.

[Full text](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8887009/)

---

### Effect of MTHFR polymorphisms on controlled ovarian stimulation: meta-analysis

**Reproductive Biology and Endocrinology**, 2021

Meta-analysis of MTHFR polymorphisms and COS outcomes, including oocyte/MII yield and pregnancy outcomes. Useful context for whether MTHFR status should inform stimulation protocol or supplementation dosing.

[Full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8490531/)

---

### Folate, MTHFR genetics, and pregnancy outcomes — review

**Frontiers in Nutrition**, February 2026

Recent review covering folate metabolism, MTHFR/MTRR polymorphisms, B12 interaction, and pregnancy outcomes. Notes that genotype-tailored supplementation has been shown to reduce miscarriage rates in some studies. Particularly relevant given the methylation analysis already in the project.

[Full text](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2026.1785263/full)
